About Me:

       I am a senior research professor of bioinformatics, brain science, and oncology at CAS (Homepage English or Chinese of CAS) and BGI-Research. Graduated from South China University of Technology with a bachelor’s degree in <Applied Physics>, and a Ph.D. in <Genetics>.

       I have been involved in numerous significant international genomics research projects, including the giant panda genome, polar bear population genomics, the Genome 10K (G10K) project, the Cancer Single Cell Project, and the Human Cell Atlas.        I have published over 50 SCI papers, with a total citation count exceeding 10,000 times and an H-index greater than 30. Among these publications, I have authored 21 papers as the primary author (first author/co-first author/corresponding author), including 6 articles in top-tier journals such as Cell, Nature, and Science.

Research Interests:

       We are dedicated to integrating interdisciplinary theoretical knowledge from physics, computer science, and mathematics to conduct research on multi-omics big data in life sciences.

       Our aim is to understand complex biological systems such as brain science, tumor immunology, and the dynamic changes in organ and function evolution across species. We welcome you to join our research group to explore the frontiers of science and promote advancements in life sciences. Join us as we embark on this exciting journey of scientific discovery!

<1>: Multi-Omics Big Data / Computational Biology. Combining genomics, single-cell analysis, spatial transcriptomics, and epigenetics, we utilize multi-omics big data to elucidate mechanisms at the molecular and cellular levels during biological processes.

<2>: Evolutionary Developmental Studies at Cellular and Functional Levels. Focusing on understanding the spatiotemporal dimensions of the central dogma (from the central dogma to spatiotemporal principles), we develop novel computational methods for studying evolutionary development at molecular, cellular, and structural levels. This includes exploring the mechanisms of tissue and organ development and investigating the principles of structure and function evolution.

<3>: Brain Science and Cancer Disease Research. We construct cell atlases of brain tissues and tumor microenvironments, study the diversity of cell types and spatial structures in the nervous and immune systems, and explore the regulatory mechanisms of cell migration and differentiation.

<4>: Comparative Genomics. Through genomic and population genetic analyses, we investigate the genetic mechanisms underlying species adaptability and changes in population structure.

Summary of scientific contributions:

       Our research group has long been engaged in the big data analysis and interpretation of multi-omics in life sciences, spanning genomics, single-cell spatial transcriptomics, and their applications in brain science, tumor immunology, and species adaptability across multiple research directions.

(1) Advances in Cellular Genomics

       In the field of cellular genomics, we have made significant progress using single-cell omics and spatial transcriptomics, particularly in studies on cell heterogeneity and evolutionary development:

       1.1. Single-cell Genomics and Transcriptomics: Our research has revealed the differentiation and evolution of cancer cells (Nature Communications 2016) and conducted a systematic comparison of different single-cell platforms (Genome Biology 2019).

       1.2. Spatial Heterogeneity of Immune Cells in Colorectal Cancer Microenvironment: We elucidated the spatial heterogeneity of immune cells within the colorectal cancer microenvironment (Clinical and Translational Medicine 2021), as well as changes in the immune microenvironment and mechanisms of drug resistance before and after treatment (Cell Reports Medicine 2023).

       1.3. Immune Cell Dynamics in Hepatocellular Carcinoma Recurrence: Our studies have examined changes in key immune cell types within the microenvironment of recurrent hepatocellular carcinoma (Cell 2021) and analyzed the spatial heterogeneity of CTCs and immune escape mechanisms during liver cancer metastasis (Nature Communications 2021).

       1.4. Mechanisms of Intrahepatic Cholangiocarcinoma Invasion: Additionally, we uncovered the mechanisms of intrahepatic cholangiocarcinoma invasion (Cell Research 2023).

(2) Progress with Spatio-temporal Omics Technologies

       With the development of new spatiotemporal omics technologies, we have achieved notable advancements in single-cell spatial transcriptomics and ultra-large-scale single-cell omics:

       2.1. Non-human Primate Telencephalon Cell Atlas: We constructed a cell atlas of the non-human primate telencephalon (Nature Communications 2022).

       2.2. Single-cell Atlas Database for Macaque Diabetes Models: Developed the first single-cell atlas database for macaque diabetes and disease models (Cell Metabolism 2024).

       2.3. Cross-species Comparative Spatial Atlas of Marmoset Cerebellum: Completed the first cross-species comparative spatial atlas of the marmoset cerebellum (Science 2024).

       2.4. Spateo Toolkit for 3D Reconstruction and Evolutionary Dynamics: Developed the Spateo toolkit for 3D reconstruction of spatial omics data and dynamic studies of developmental evolution (Cell 2024).

(3) Early Achievements in Animal Genomics

       In the early stages of our research, we achieved important results in animal genome assembly, annotation, and evolutionary studies:

       3.1. Genome Drafts of Various Species: We drafted the genomes of the giant panda (Nature 2010), pig roundworm (Nature 2011), Egyptian schistosome (Nature Genetics 2012), and cheetah (Genome Biology 2015).

       3.2. Key Positive Selection Genes in Polar Bears: Through genomic and population resequencing techniques, we identified key positively selected genes and population structure characteristics that allow polar bears to adapt to polar climates (Cell 2014).

       3.3. Distribution of Retrotransposons in Chinese Populations: Using high-depth population resequencing, we studied the distribution features of retrotransposons in Chinese populations (GigaScience 2017).
